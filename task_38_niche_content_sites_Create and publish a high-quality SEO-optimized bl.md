Let’s talk about something that doesn’t get nearly enough attention—despite affecting millions of lives. India’s financial inclusion story is one we’ve all heard before: 380 million new bank accounts, digital payments lighting up villages, Jan Dhan Yojana being hailed as a global model. On paper, it’s a triumph. But in reality? For many of the poorest and most vulnerable, inclusion has come at a brutal cost.

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: access to finance doesn’t mean fair finance. In fact, for far too many, it’s opened the door to predatory lending dressed up as empowerment.

Take microfinance. Once seen as a lifeline for women entrepreneurs and low-income households, it’s now morphing into something darker. The Reserve Bank of India’s own data reveals the average interest rate on microfinance loans sits at 24.4%—more than double the national average for formal credit. Let that sink in. These aren’t subprime borrowers in a developed economy; these are people surviving on razor-thin margins, scraping together a few hundred rupees a day. And they’re being charged more than credit card companies would dare charge in Mumbai or Delhi.

This isn’t just high interest—it’s systemic extraction.

Now, who’s behind this? Blaming "the system" is easy. But the real players are clear. Yes, Non-Banking Financial Companies—NBFCs—bear significant responsibility. They dominate the microfinance space, often operating in regulatory gray zones with minimal oversight. Their business models thrive on volume, not sustainability. Aggressive field agents, doorstep collections, social pressure within self-help groups—these aren’t anomalies. They’re features.

But let’s not let regulators off the hook. The RBI sets guidelines, yet enforcement remains inconsistent. There’s a cap—24%—but the average is *already* at 24.4%. That means someone, somewhere, is clearly gaming the rules. And when penalties are weak or delayed, compliance becomes optional.

Worse still, the government’s push for financial inclusion hasn’t been matched with a parallel push for financial literacy or consumer protection. We handed people bank accounts and UPI IDs but didn’t teach them how to spot a bad loan when it’s wrapped in a smile and a sari. That’s not inclusion. That’s exposure.

The human cost? Devastating.

IIMA research shows the typical microfinance borrower spends over half their income just servicing debt. Half. Imagine trying to feed your family, send your kids to school, afford medicine—all after giving 50% of what you earn to a lender. It’s not budgeting. It’s surrender.

And once you’re in, it’s hard to get out. When repayment pressure mounts, borrowers don’t walk away—they take another loan. Sometimes from a different NBFC, sometimes from a local moneylender charging 60%, even 100%. This isn’t borrowing. It’s financial quicksand.

I’ve spoken with women in rural Rajasthan who took a 20,000-rupee loan to expand a tiny grocery stall. Within two years, they owed 75,000—on the same original amount—after rolling over payments and taking top-up loans. “They said it was easy,” one told me. “No one explained the math.”

That’s the crux: financial products without transparency are weapons.

So what do we do?

First, let’s stop treating financial inclusion as a checkbox. It’s not enough to open an account. We need *responsible* inclusion—products designed for dignity, not debt dependency. That means:

Know your rights. The RBI mandates fair practices—caps on interest, transparent fee structures, no coercive recovery. If your lender isn’t following them, report them. Your district’s banking ombudsman isn’t a ghost office—it’s there for you.

Diversify your income. Relying on one stream makes you fragile. I’ve seen women turn home kitchens into small catering businesses, stitch masks during pandemics, or resell digital data packs locally. Micro-entrepreneurship isn’t glamorous, but it builds resilience.

Build a buffer. Even 50 rupees a week adds up. Aim for three to six months of expenses in a safe, accessible account. It’s not about getting rich—it’s about not having to borrow when the roof leaks or the baby gets sick.

Say no to multiple loans. That “top-up” offer? It’s rarely your friend. Consolidate if you must, but do it wisely—through a formal channel with lower rates, not another informal loop.

And if you’re drowning? Ask for help. There are NGOs, credit counseling services, and even some fintechs now offering debt management tools. You’re not weak for struggling. You’re human.

Here’s what gives me hope: the same technology that enabled rapid lending can now empower borrowers. Imagine an app that instantly compares microfinance terms, flags violations, or even simulates repayment scenarios in local languages. That’s not sci-fi—it’s possible. And it’s overdue.

We also need stronger accountability. Regulators must move faster, penalize violations harder, and demand transparency—not just from NBFCs, but from the fintechs partnering with them. No more hiding behind algorithms or third-party agents when things go wrong.

And to the policymakers: inclusion without protection is just another form of exploitation. The next phase of this journey must be about financial *justice*, not just access.

Because let’s be honest—this isn’t just about interest rates. It’s about power. Who holds it, who abuses it, and who gets left holding the bill.

We can do better. We must.

Financial freedom shouldn’t be a slogan on a government pamphlet. It should be real. Tangible. Available to the woman stitching clothes at 2 a.m. to pay off a loan she barely understood when she signed.

She’s not just a statistic. She’s the litmus test of our financial system’s integrity.

And right now, that test is being failed.

So let’s shift the narrative. Let’s celebrate not just account numbers, but fair terms. Not just loan disbursals, but debt-free graduations. Let’s build a system where inclusion doesn’t mean exploitation in disguise.

The tools are there. The awareness is growing. Now we need the will.

Because financial dignity isn’t a privilege. It’s a right.
